**Meeting type:** Campaign reasoning discovery — informal
**Relationship:** 14-year professional relationship. Kaye sees Benet as the answer to her problems.
**Goal:** Extract the campaign reasoning that fills the discovery worksheet. This is the real discovery conversation.
**Note:** Record the meeting. The transcript fills the Campaign Brief Workflow Discovery Categories 1-3.

---

## What This Conversation Is

Kaye is the domain expert. She holds the campaign reasoning — the whys, the assumptions, the tradeoffs, the claim boundaries, the lessons. Jeremy confirmed all campaigns roll up to her.

This is not a pitch meeting. This is not rapport-building (you have 14 years of that). This is the conversation where you get the actual answers that have been TBD in every planning artifact so far.

You don't need to frame, justify, or explain the architecture. Kaye already trusts you. Just ask and listen.

## What You Need From Kaye

### Pick one campaign to walk through

Either a recent campaign she remembers well, or one that's opening now. The Jeremy Renner campaign is a natural candidate since both you and Jeremy referenced it — but let Kaye pick. She'll choose the one she can speak to most fully.

### Then get the reasoning behind it

Not the brief itself. The thinking that produced the brief.

## Questions

These are conversational, not a checklist. Follow Kaye's thread. But make sure you leave with answers to these.

### The Campaign

- Which campaign are we talking about?
- What was it actually for — what was the real goal?
- Was there a secondary goal nobody said out loud?
- Who was the target audience and how did you pick them?

### The Brief

- What did the brief look like? Walk me through it.
- What sections matter most?
- Which parts were you confident about and which parts were you guessing on?
- Did anything get copied forward from a prior campaign without being re-examined?
- What was vague but you moved forward anyway?

### The Sources

- What did you actually check or reference while putting this together?
- Which of those sources do you trust and which ones are "better than nothing"?
- What sources do you wish existed but don't?
- Is there anything critical that lives in someone's head and has never been written down?

### The Claims and Boundaries

- Were there any claims you wanted to make but couldn't — or shouldn't have?
- What brand or compliance rules shaped what you could say?
- Did anyone review the claims before it went out?

### The Assumptions

- What were you assuming about the audience?
- What were you assuming about the message?
- Looking back, were any of those assumptions wrong?
- Which assumptions were tested and which were just accepted?

### The Stopping Point

- What made the brief feel done enough to go?
- Was there anything you still weren't sure about when you approved it?
- If you had one more week, what would you have investigated?

### The Outcome

- How did it perform?
- What surprised you?
- Did any of the results contradict what you expected?

### The Learning

- What did you learn from this one?
- Did that learning change how you approached the next campaign?
- If someone started a similar campaign tomorrow, what would you tell them?
- Is any of that written down, or does it live in your head?

### The Gap

- What reasoning do you have to re-explain every time a new campaign starts?
- What do new people on the team not know that they should?
- What information do you wish just surfaced automatically when starting a brief?

### The Prototype

- If I could build something that preserved the reasoning behind a campaign brief so the next one started smarter — what would that need to do?
- What should it absolutely not do?

## What Not To Do

- Don't explain the framework unless she asks
- Don't show the paper
- Don't walk through architecture diagrams
- Don't ask her to validate the HLD
- Don't pitch — she's already bought in
- Don't ask about Martin's infrastructure (that's a separate conversation)
