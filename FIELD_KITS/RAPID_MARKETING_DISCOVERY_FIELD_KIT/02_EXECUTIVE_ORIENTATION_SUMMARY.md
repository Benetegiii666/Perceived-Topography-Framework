# Why We Are Studying Campaign Brief Reasoning

*Source: adapted from `COMPANION_ARTIFACTS/EXECUTIVE_BRIEF_v1.0.md` and `08_PAPER_DRAFTS/PAPER_v1.0_WORKING.md` (abstract).*

---

## The Short Version

Teams usually preserve the final artifact — the campaign brief, the report, the approved messaging — but often lose the reasoning that produced it.

Valuable reasoning lives in scattered places: documents, Slack messages, prior campaigns, sales feedback, brand rules, claim boundaries, personal experience, and meeting memory. When the next campaign starts, teams may have to rediscover the same assumptions, constraints, evidence boundaries, and lessons.

The goal of this work is not to judge the team or replace human judgment. The goal is to understand how campaign decisions actually form so that a lightweight prototype could help preserve useful reasoning across cycles.

---

## What We Mean by "Reasoning"

Not just what was decided, but:

- What was the goal of the campaign?
- What assumptions shaped the targeting, messaging, and claims?
- What sources did the team trust, and how confident were they?
- What claims required approval or evidence?
- What made the brief feel "good enough" to move forward?
- What did the campaign later teach the team?
- Did that learning carry into the next campaign — or did the next team start from scratch?

---

## Why the Campaign Brief Workflow

The first focus is the campaign brief workflow because it is:

- **Recurring** — briefs are produced regularly, not once.
- **Decision-heavy** — the brief commits the team to audience, message, claims, and approach.
- **Outcome-visible** — campaign performance provides measurable feedback.
- **Reasoning-lossy** — the brief survives, but the reasoning often does not.

---

## What the Prototype Would Do

The prototype idea is to help future campaign work start from preserved reasoning rather than a cold prompt or blank page:

- When the next campaign starts, the system retrieves prior reasoning — not just prior documents, but the goals, assumptions, confidence levels, evidence boundaries, and lessons from earlier cycles.
- The system proposes a draft interpretation of the new campaign's goal, assumptions, and constraints based on what it retrieves.
- A human reviews and confirms, corrects, or rejects the interpretation before the system acts.
- After the campaign runs, outcomes are compared to expectations. If something was wrong, the team identifies what assumption failed and whether the lesson should shape future campaigns.

The system does the reasoning work first. The human confirms only what matters.
