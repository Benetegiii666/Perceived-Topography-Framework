## 1. Overall Verdict

**PASS WITH TARGETED READER-EXPERIENCE EDITS**

The paper reads as one argument. The spine lands. Every section earns the next. Transitions are not merely structural — they are logical advances where each section's closing question becomes the next section's opening problem.

The manuscript is not a set of strong sections stitched together. It is a single argumentative arc that moves from fear through mechanism through practice. A reader who begins at Section 1 is carried through to "Build better landscapes" without needing to restart, re-orient, or wonder why a section exists.

Five targeted edits would strengthen the reader experience. None are structural. None require rewriting sections. All are small, high-leverage interventions.

## 3. Section-by-Section Reader Journey

### S1 — From Agent Fear to Landscape Design

**What it does well:** Opens with a concrete problem (agent misbehavior in safety evaluations), refuses the moralization frame, and pivots to the landscape question within ~600 words. The marble analogy is vivid and accessible. The gradient and sufficiency definitions now land early (line 80–82) thanks to the sharpening edits. The thesis ("the easiest sufficient path should also be the desired path") is clear and memorable.

**Where friction appears:** The section is long (~1,600 words). For a fast reader, the healthcare campaign preview (lines 90–100) may feel like too much detail before the reader has the framework to understand it. A Cassidy-type reader might wish the section arrived at its thesis faster.

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — "context alone does not preserve the why" is the right bridge.

### S2 — Context Does Not Preserve the Why

**What it does well:** The strongest conceptual distinction in the paper. "Context tells the system what information is available. It does not necessarily tell the system why that information matters." This lands immediately and creates genuine intellectual tension. The knowledge-reuse vs. reasoning-reuse contrast is sharp.

**Where friction appears:** Minimal. The section is well-paced and uses the healthcare campaign as illustration without over-explaining it.

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — "what must be preserved first?" is the natural next question.

### S3 — Optimizer State and Perceived Topography

**What it does well:** Introduces optimizer state with appropriate disclaimers. The five topography dimensions are introduced with a compact, memorable structure. The Dimension Admission Matrix (Table 1) is one of the paper's most useful tables — it shows how the dimensions were selected and how candidates were evaluated.

**Where friction appears:** This is the paper's densest section. It introduces Goal, Policy, Interpretation, then five dimensions, then the admission matrix, then the policy/memory distinction. A practitioner reader may feel overloaded. The section could benefit from a one-sentence signpost after the optimizer-state introduction: "These three elements form the working frame. The landscape they move through is shaped by five dimensions."

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — "landscape alone does not explain movement" is the right pivot.

### S4 — Gradients, Sufficiency, and Failure

**What it does well:** The paper's most important theoretical section. Attraction → Investigation → Sufficiency → Action is a clean motion model. The sufficiency ≠ certainty distinction (Table 2) is excellent. The premature sufficiency comparison (Table 3) is the paper's strongest diagnostic tool. The healthcare "reduces readmissions" example makes the abstract concrete.

**Where friction appears:** Moderate density. The section introduces four named concepts (gradient, attraction, investigation, sufficiency) plus premature sufficiency plus the motion model. A reader who skims may miss the gradient/attraction distinction. However, the concrete examples (especially the "reduces readmissions" walkthrough) carry the weight.

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — "placed into a complete campaign workflow, the difference becomes visible" leads naturally to S5.

### S5 — A Constructed Stress Test

**What it does well:** The paper's most vivid section. The context-only vs. reasoning-state comparison is executed with exceptional detail. The reader can feel the premature sufficiency happening in the context-only path. The reasoning-state path is not presented as magic — it is bounded, uncertain, and human-dependent. Table 4 is a powerful summary device.

**Where friction appears:** The section is long (~3,800 words). Some readers may find the dual-path walkthrough exhausting. However, the detail is what makes the comparison credible. This is a section where length serves the argument.

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — "memory can retrieve the old material; learning changes the reasoning state from which the next action begins" is the right bridge.

### S6 — Learning Requires Preserved Reasoning

**What it does well:** The four-way distinction (correction, reaction, postmortem, storage vs. learning) is one of the paper's most disciplined conceptual moves. Each "not learning" example is concrete and memorable. The Model Update Object is introduced naturally as a consequence of the argument, not as a specification imposed on the reader.

**Where friction appears:** The section is long (~2,400 words). The four "not learning" subsections are individually strong but cumulatively heavy. A fast reader may feel the point has been made after two examples.

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — "that problem is Discovery" is one of the paper's best bridge sentences.

### S7 — Discovery Turns Human Intent Into Reusable Reasoning

**What it does well:** The compressed-intent examples (campaign, customer update, summary, approval path) are vivid and varied. The Retrieve → Infer → Propose → Confirm loop feels natural and implementable. The burden-reduction argument is strong. Table 6 is effective. The governance humility is well-calibrated.

**Where friction appears:** The section is long (~3,800 words). The Propose subsection includes four full worked proposals across four domains — this is vivid but takes space. The tool-action callback adds value but extends the section further.

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — "what kind of system can hold these reasoning surfaces?" is the right structural question.

### S8 — What It Would Take to Build This

**What it does well:** The "unit of organizational reasoning is not the document — it is the optimizer state transition" is the paper's strongest architectural claim. The six-object table (Table 7) introduces each object through what fails without it. The maturity model (Table 8) is diagnostic, not prescriptive. The lifecycle is stated as a capability requirement, not a workflow specification.

**Where friction appears:** Moderate. The individual-object failure paragraphs after Table 7 partially repeat the table's "what fails without it" column. However, they add vivid detail. The section walks the line between conceptual architecture and product specification — it mostly stays on the right side.

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — "what would make the framework wrong?" is the right next question.

### S9 — Boundaries, Objections, and Falsifiability

**What it does well:** The paper's strongest credibility section. The opening standard ("a framework that explains everything explains nothing") sets a high bar and the section meets it. The three objections are handled with genuine concession-and-sharpening. Table 9 (disconfirmation conditions) is concrete. The governance/measurement correction paragraphs are important and well-placed.

**Where friction appears:** Minimal. The section reads like the framework walking into cross-examination.

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — the implicit question after "contact with evidence" is "where does this framework come from?"

### S10 — The Blend Has Roots

**What it does well:** Compact and effective. Table 10 compresses 15 traditions into 12 rows. The "contribution is the blend, not the grapes" is memorable. The five synthesis contributions are specific. The closing creates the need for S11 without becoming the conclusion.

**Where friction appears:** Minimal. The section is well-paced at ~1,200 words.

**Does it earn the next section?** Yes — "the remaining choice is whether builders shape those landscapes accidentally or deliberately."

### S11 — Build Better Landscapes

**What it does well:** The prompting → loops → landscapes contrast is the paper's sharpest practical reframe. Figure 8 crystallizes the assembled framework. The healthcare campaign runs through the mechanism compactly. The closing sequence (S1 callback → "Build better landscapes") lands as earned imperative.

**Where friction appears:** None significant. The section is well-paced at ~1,050 words.

**Does it earn Appendix A?** Yes — the reader has seen the mechanism and wants to know how to start.

### Appendix A — The Decision Topography Interview

**What it does well:** Plain-language questions that practitioners can answer Monday morning. The three-part structure (what/why/what will this become) bridges interview to architecture. The architecture-landing table shows the translation. The minimal variant provides a fast start.

**Where friction appears:** One formatting issue in the Minimal Interview Variant table — row 2 appears to be missing the "Map" phase label in the Phase column. This should be verified.

## 8. Top 5 Recommended Narrative Edits

### Edit 1 — Appendix A Minimal Variant Table Row 2 Phase Column

**Location:** Appendix A, Minimal Interview Variant table, row 2
**Problem:** Row 2 ("What are we acting as though we already know?") appears to be missing the "Map" phase label in the Phase column based on the transition zone read.
**Proposed fix:** Verify and add "Map" to the Phase column if missing.
**Severity:** Required before external review — broken table formatting.

### Edit 2 — S3 Signpost After Optimizer State Introduction

**Location:** S3, after the optimizer-state definition (~line 218–220 area)
**Problem:** The section moves from defining Goal/Policy/Interpretation to the five dimensions without a clear signpost. A fast reader may not realize the section has two halves.
**Proposed fix:** Add one sentence after the optimizer-state primitives are introduced: "These three elements form the working frame. The next question is what kind of landscape that frame moves through." (~15 words)
**Severity:** Recommended before external review — smooths the densest section.

### Edit 3 — S5 Length Awareness

**Location:** S5 (entire section, ~3,800 words)
**Problem:** The section is the paper's longest. The dual-path walkthrough is vivid but may exhaust some readers.
**Proposed fix:** Not a cut — but consider adding a one-sentence signpost at the section opening: "This section is the paper's longest because it is the paper's most important test." Or simply accept the length as earned.
**Severity:** Optional polish — the length is justified by the argument.

### Edit 4 — S6 "Not Learning" Subsection Compression

**Location:** S6, the four "not learning" subsections (~lines 960–1110)
**Problem:** By the third "not learning" example (postmortem), the pattern is clear. The fourth (storage) partially repeats earlier arguments.
**Proposed fix:** Consider compressing the storage subsection by ~30%. The point ("storage does not decide among these interpretations") can be made in fewer sentences.
**Severity:** Optional polish — each subsection adds value, but the sequence is heavy.

### Edit 5 — S8 Object Failure Paragraphs

**Location:** S8, the six individual-object failure paragraphs after Table 7 (~lines 1690–1740)
**Problem:** Table 7's "What fails without it" column already makes each object's necessity visible. The subsequent paragraphs partially repeat this.
**Proposed fix:** Consider cutting the paragraphs for the two least-differentiated objects (Learning Event and Model Update Object — both are already well-established from S6). Keep the paragraphs for Optimizer State, Premise Stack, Decision State, and Investigation Trace, which add genuinely new examples.
**Severity:** Optional polish — the paragraphs add vivid detail but the table already carries the core argument.
