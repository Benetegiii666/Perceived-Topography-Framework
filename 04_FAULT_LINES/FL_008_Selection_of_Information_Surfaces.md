# FL_008: Selection of Information Surfaces

**Status:** Open — emerging tension
**Date:** 2026-06-09
**Source:** Marketing knowledge platform example (07_NARRATIVE_ARCHITECTURE/EXAMPLES.md)

## The Tension

The framework explains how information surfaces influence perception construction. It does not yet explain **how information surfaces are selected**.

In the marketing example, Scenario A and Scenario B expose different subsets of reality. The framework can explain why different surfaces produce different perceptions. But it cannot yet explain:

- Why Scenario A was chosen over Scenario B (or vice versa)
- What principles should govern that choice
- Whether selection is itself a form of governance, optimization, or something else

## Key Question

**What governing principles determine which information should be surfaced for a decision-maker?**

## Candidate Selection Principles

1. **Relevance** — Surface information that relates to the decision at hand
2. **Success prediction** — Surface information that improves the likelihood of a good outcome
3. **Uncertainty reduction** — Surface information that reduces the decision-maker's uncertainty
4. **Decision impact** — Surface information that would change the decision if known

These are not mutually exclusive, and they may conflict. Relevance-optimized surfaces may omit high-impact information that appears unrelated. Success-prediction surfaces may create confirmation bias. Uncertainty-reduction surfaces may paralyze through information overload.

## Why This Matters

If information surfaces are a distinct architectural layer (FL_007, trending toward Outcome B or C), then the **design of those surfaces becomes a primary lever for shaping behavior**. But design requires selection criteria. Without them, the framework describes the mechanism but cannot guide its use.

This is the gap between descriptive and prescriptive — which is also Open Question #4.

## Architectural Implications

- If selection is a form of **governance**, it connects to FL_004 (Governance vs Topography) and ST-3 (Governance as Environmental Design). Governance doesn't just shape topography — it curates information surfaces.
- If selection is a form of **optimization**, then there's an optimizer *above* the information surface layer — which complicates the hierarchy.
- If selection is **emergent** (no one designs it; it arises from organizational structure), then information surface quality is a function of organizational topography. The framework becomes recursive.
